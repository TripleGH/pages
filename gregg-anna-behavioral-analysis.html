Two people can be behaviorally complementary and yet fundamentally misaligned on what they're building toward. Or they can be behaviorally opposite and deeply united in purpose. Love Languages & Intimacy Needs
Nothing in these profiles addresses how you each feel loved, what physical affection means to you, how you express desire, what "connection" actually looks like in practice. How This Data Will NOT Be Helpful

What These Profiles Are

These are behavioral assessments — likely based on the PDP (Professional DynaMetric Programs) or a similar DISC-adjacent framework. They measure observable behavioral tendencies along 4-5 dimensions. They are self-reported. They were assessed on a single day (Anna: Jan 9, 2026; Gregg: Feb 11, 2026). They are descriptive, not prescriptive.

Specifically, Do Not Use This Data To...

  • Predict specific outcomes. "Gregg is a Maverick" does not mean he will always act boldly. These are tendencies, not determinism. On any given Tuesday, either of you might behave completely out of character.
  • Win an argument. "Your profile says you avoid conflict" is not a valid point in a disagreement. Using assessment data as ammunition will destroy trust faster than any behavioral mismatch.
  • Excuse behavior. "That's just how I am" is never a complete sentence when it follows hurting someone. The profiles describe defaults, not ceilings.
  • Replace actual conversation. These reports are a map. The territory is the lived experience of your relationship. If the map says one thing and reality says another, trust reality.
  • Diagnose clinical issues. These are not psychological assessments. They don't measure anxiety, depression, ADHD, trauma response, or any clinical dimension. If something feels bigger than "behavioral style," get professional support.
  • Assess compatibility. There is no valid "compatibility score" derivable from these profiles. Complementary profiles can thrive or implode. Similar profiles can mesh or bore each other. The work of the relationship determines outcomes, not the starting positions.

The Self-Concept Gap (Both Profiles Show One)

Both profiles include a "Self" graph and a "Self-Concept" graph. The Self is how you actually behave. The Self-Concept is how you think you behave — your aspirational or idealized self-image. Where these diverge is interesting:

  • Gregg's gap: His Self-Concept shows himself slightly more collaborative and more sociable than his actual behavior scores. He may believe he's more of a team player than he actually comes across as.
  • Anna's gap: Her Self-Concept shows herself slightly more independent and sociable than her behavior. She may aspire to more assertiveness and social ease than she currently demonstrates.

These gaps are growth edges. They show where each person is already reaching — and where a supportive partner can help.

The Practical Playbook

Communication Protocols

For Gregg → Anna: Set up a time to talk rather than dropping by spontaneously. Let her think before responding. Don't interpret silence as agreement or disinterest. Ask "What's your take on this?" and then actually wait. When she offers a concern, treat it as a gift, not a speed bump.
For Anna → Gregg: Don't assume he knows you disagree if you haven't said it out loud. Be direct even when it feels uncomfortable — he actually respects directness. If you need time to think, say "I need to think about this" rather than going quiet. Make the effort to ask him for his feedback or opinions, too.
Both: Be clear on action items and next steps at the end of every important conversation. Gregg focuses on the goal; Anna focuses on the plan. Name both.

Decision-Making Framework

Given your profiles, here's a decision-making structure that plays to both strengths:

  1. Gregg opens: Frames the opportunity, the vision, the "why now." This is where his pattern-recognition and urgency add value.
  2. Anna pressure-tests: Asks the questions Gregg's profile says he'll skip — What are the risks? What's the plan? What evidence do we have? This is not foot-dragging; this is quality control.
  3. Joint decision: Agree on a go/no-go with a defined scope. Gregg's instinct is to go big; Anna's is to go safe. The right answer is usually somewhere between.
  4. Gregg drives execution broadly; Anna manages details and follow-through. Gregg delegates details — Anna is the person he should delegate to (or at least consult with on quality).

Conflict Resolution

Your specific risk pattern: Gregg pushes, Anna retreats, the issue goes underground, resentment builds. Here's the override:

The 24-Hour Rule: If either of you notices a topic being avoided, it gets scheduled (not ambushed) within 24 hours. Anna gets a heads-up so she can prepare. Gregg gets a defined time so he doesn't stew. The conversation starts with "Here's what I'm feeling" not "Here's what you did."
