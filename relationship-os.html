What This Means

The biggest gap: Formality. Nearly 3 standard deviations apart. Gregg improvises; Anna plans. This is your superpower when respected — and your biggest friction source when it's not.


The second gap: Dominance. Gregg naturally drives the agenda. Anna naturally defers. Left unchecked, Anna's voice disappears from decisions.


Where you align: Patience. You're both in a similar zone here — you can match pace when the topic matters. This is your common ground.

Friction Early-Warning

Predictable patterns — catch them early

1Speed vs. Thoroughness

▾
What it looks like

Gregg pushes to move fast. Anna wants more time. Gregg gets impatient. Anna feels steamrolled. Issue goes underground.

Early signal

Gregg starts making decisions without consulting Anna. Anna stops offering input.

The override

Gregg asks "What do you need to feel good about this?" — Anna gives a specific answer with a timeline.

2Agenda Dominance

▾
What it looks like

Gregg sets the topics, drives the conversation, Anna's concerns never make it to the table.

Early signal

Anna stops bringing things up. Or brings them up once and doesn't follow up.

The override

Gregg literally asks: "What's on YOUR mind this week that we haven't talked about?"

3The Quiet Withdrawal

▾
What it looks like

Anna gets overwhelmed and starts shortening conversations, avoiding topics, or going through the motions.

Early signal

Conversations get shorter. Anna's answers get more generic. Less eye contact, less initiation.

The override

Gregg notices and doesn't push harder. Instead: "I feel like you might be pulling back. Am I reading that right?"

4Sensitive Topic Freeze

▾
What it looks like

Something important needs discussing and neither person brings it up because both are overly cautious.

Early signal

Both people are thinking about the same thing but neither is saying it.

The override

The 24-Hour Rule. If either person notices avoidance, it gets scheduled within 24 hours. Start with "Here's what I'm feeling."

5Detail Dismissal

▾
What it looks like

Gregg's follow-up is quick and cursory. Anna expected thoroughness. Quality suffers.

Early signal

Anna starts checking Gregg's work silently or stops delegating.

The override

Be explicit upfront about what level of detail each task requires. Name it before starting.

Gregg's Turn

Frame the Opportunity

Share the vision — the "why now," the upside, the big picture. Present it as an idea to explore, not a decision already made.

Anna's Turn

Pressure-Test It

Ask the questions Gregg's wiring naturally skips: What are the risks? What's the plan? What evidence do we have? What could go wrong?

Together

Agree on go/no-go with a defined scope. The right compromise usually feels slightly uncomfortable for both — that means it's balanced.

Communication Playbook

For Gregg → Anna
Schedule, don't ambush. Anna processes internally. Say "I want to talk about X — can we sit down tonight?"
For Gregg → Anna
Let her finish. Your pace is faster. If you fill every silence, Anna stops trying. Ask, then wait.
For Gregg → Anna
Don't read silence as agreement. Check: "What's your honest take on this?" And mean it.
For Gregg → Anna
Her thoroughness is a feature. When Anna asks "but what about...?" — that's her protecting you both.
For Anna → Gregg
Be direct. Hinting won't work. His attention is forward-focused. Say the thing.
For Anna → Gregg
Lead with the big picture. Start with why this matters before the details.
For Anna → Gregg
Name it when you're withdrawing. "I need to think — can we come back tomorrow?" is better than going quiet.
For Both
End every big conversation with clear next steps. "We're aiming for X and the next step is Y by Z date."
For Both
Negotiate communication volume proactively. Some weeks need daily check-ins. Some need space. Agree on it.
